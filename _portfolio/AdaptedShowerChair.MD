## Introduction

The project was designed to help Miyako, a 9 year old girl with muscular distrophy. Recently, she underwent surgery to fuse her neck and place rods in her back. This improved her posture, but it limited her head movement. Before this project, Miyako was unable to independly shower, relying on her parents to assist her. The goal of the project was to design a shower chair that fit her and gave her enough support to be more independent in her daily activities. Many of the supportive shower chairs that were available on the market were either too small (toddler sized), or they were too big (adult sized). <br><br>

This project was a part of the 2025 Vanderbilt TOM Makeathon Event (10/24/25 - 10/26/25).

## Design

The foundation of this chair was a shower chair purchased off of Amazon (LINK HERE). This specific chair allowed the leg height to be adjusted, which was perfect as it would allow the chair to grow with Miyako. <br><br>

To fabricate the supports, it was decided that PVC piping would be ideal. It is sturdy enough to support weight, and it is water resistant. Pool noodles then covered the PVC pipes where Miyako make contact with the back and arm supports to make the the seat more comfortable. <br><br>

Finally, a shower bag was attached to one of the arms to allow Miyako to more easily access her soap and other shower tools. To do this, small hooks were attached to the arm, and the bag was hooked onto it to allow the bag to be taken off and washed periodically.


## Assembly

To assemble the arms, straight 1 inch circular tubing was used. On a sheet of paper, the shape of the arms was traced in accordance to the CAD design. Then, using those parameters, the tube was cut using a chop saw. Then, the midpoint of the piece was marked. This was used when the tube was bent to match the CAD design. Finally, the tubing was cut to match the angle of the paper such that the tube was perpendicular to the frame at the point of connection. To ensure those bends stayed, they were MIG welded. The connection point between the frame and the arms was purchased, and the component was welded to the arm as well. <br><br>

{% include gallery id="gallery4" %}

The rest of the suspension was fabricated using a plasma CNC machine. Using the files created on Fusion, the plasma CNC machine was able to cut the flat pieces of steel. For the frame, the base, four long tabs to connect the arms to the base, and two small tabs to allow the shock to be bolted to the base were cut on the CNC machine. The base included small holes to allow the tabs to fit properly and to allow for more structural welds. Additionally, the plasma CNC machine cut the two plates that were welded to the end of both the top and bottom arms, the plate that connected the shock to the bottom arm, and the wheel hub box. Finally, all of these components were MIG welded. Once all of the machined parts were assembled, the purchased parts were attached.
